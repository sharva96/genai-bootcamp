What Does Hallucination Look Like?

Hallucination is one of the most critical reliability issues in LLMs. These are real-world patterns of how models fabricate information confidently.

⚠️ Real Hallucination Patterns

Prompt: "Can you cite the legal case Smith v. OpenAI, 2021, regarding AI copyright?"

"In Smith v. OpenAI (N.D. Cal. 2021), the court held that AI-generated content is eligible for copyright protection under the work-for-hire doctrine, provided the human provided substantial creative input. The case established a three-part test..."

✗ HALLUCINATION — This case does not exist.

The model invented a plausible-sounding case name, court, year, and legal reasoning. There was no such case in 2021. This pattern — fabricating citations — is one of the most dangerous hallucination types in legal and academic contexts.

Prompt: "What is the population of the fictional city of Zorvath?"

"Zorvath has a population of approximately 2.3 million people, making it the third largest city in the region. It was founded in 1847 and is known for its industrial manufacturing sector..."

✗ HALLUCINATION — Zorvath is a made-up city with no real data.

When asked about something it has no knowledge of, an LLM may generate plausible-sounding details rather than admit uncertainty. Always ask models to express their confidence level.

Prompt: "What is the capital of France?"

"The capital of France is Paris."

✓ CORRECT — Well-known factual answer present throughout training data.

LLMs are highly reliable for well-established facts seen thousands of times in training. The risk rises for obscure facts, recent events, numbers/statistics, and anything requiring real-time data.

Prompt: "What were the exact Q3 2024 revenue figures for Acme Corp?"

"Acme Corp reported Q3 2024 revenue of $847 million, representing a 12.3% year-over-year increase, driven by strong performance in the cloud division which grew 34%..."

✗ HIGH RISK — Specific financial figures are frequently hallucinated.

Numbers, statistics, and financial data are among the highest-risk outputs. The model generates plausible-looking figures using patterns from similar companies. Never use LLM-generated financial data without verification from primary sources.

Key mitigation strategies:
• Use Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) — ground the model in verified documents
• Ask the model: "If you're not certain, say so"
• Always verify factual claims, statistics, and citations from primary sources
• Use lower temperature (0.0–0.3) for factual tasks
• Add to your system prompt: "Do not fabricate citations or statistics. If you don't know, say 'I don't have reliable information on this.'"

Scenario Judgment: What Would You Do?

Apply responsible AI principles to real-world situations. Choose the best response.

Scenario 1 — Healthcare
A hospital deploys an AI chatbot to help patients understand their diagnosis reports. The chatbot is powered by GPT-4o and provides detailed medical explanations. A patient receives a response saying their test results "may indicate early-stage pancreatic cancer" and becomes extremely distressed — but their doctor says the AI misinterpreted a routine marker.

Which responsible AI principle was most violated, and what should the hospital have done?

Scenario 2 — HR Tool
A company uses an AI tool to screen resumes for software engineering roles. They train the model on the last 10 years of successful hires. An audit reveals the model consistently scores resumes from women and candidates from certain universities 20% lower — even with identical qualifications.

What is the root cause and the correct remediation?

Scenario 3 — Customer Data
A law firm wants to use ChatGPT to summarize client legal briefs to save 3 hours per case. The attorney plans to paste full client documents (including names, case details, and confidential strategy) directly into the chat interface.

What is the primary risk, and what should the firm do instead?
